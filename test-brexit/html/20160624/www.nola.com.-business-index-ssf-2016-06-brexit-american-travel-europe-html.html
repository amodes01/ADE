What Brexit means for your European vacation

  • Jennifer Larino, NOLA.com | The Times-Picayune
  • Jun 24, 2016
  • Jun 24, 2016 Updated Jul 19, 2019
  • 2 min to read
  • Facebook
  • Twitter
  • WhatsApp
  • SMS
  • Email

The United Kingdom's vote to leave the European Union is still being dissected by experts trying to understand how the economic consequences could play out over coming years. For American travelers, a more immediate question comes to mind: How will Brexit affect my European vacation?

The answer is a mixed bag. A stronger U.S. dollar could make travel cheaper. Airfare between the U.K. and European countries could also go up.

Here is what we know so far about what Brexit means for Americans traveling to Europe.

Travel to the U.K. could get cheaper.

The Huffington Post reports the value of the British pound hit a 30-year low in the wake of the Brexit vote. That's big news for a currency that has historically been very strong against the U.S. dollar. See a historical chart comparing the currencies.

One British pound is currently worth $1.37 in American dollars, down from $1.50 earlier this week. Travel to Britain will get much cheaper for Americans if the value of the pound continues to fall and the value of the U.S. dollar gets stronger.

Travel to Europe could get cheaper, too.

CNN Money reports the value of the euro has also fallen, though not as rapidly. If the downward trend continues, Americans could get more bang for their buck while traveling within in countries inside the European "euro zone."

Bloomberg reports lower spending power may also prompt U.K. residents to scale back vacations to Europe in coming years. That could mean to better deals to destinations favored by British travelers such as Ibiza or Tuscany.

Airfare from the U.K. to Europe could go up.

The Brexit vote could make airfare pricier for American travelers who book so-called "puddle jumper" flights between the U.K. and cities in Europe. The Telegraph reports the European Union helped dismantle old restrictions on air travel on the continent and usher in a new era of competitive, low cost routes between countries in the union.

New air service agreements will have to be negotiated, which could restrict how freely - and cheaply -- airlines such as Britain's easyJet and Ireland's Ryanair operate between the U.K. and Europe, the report says. That said, now is still a good time to search for hotel and flights deals to Europe.

Americans traveling to the U.K. could see longer customs lines.

Citizens within the European Union enter a separate expedited line when traveling between union countries, including when arriving at London's Heathrow airport. The Brexit vote means EU residents could now join Americans and other international travelers in a single customs line, which could mean longer wait times.

Charlie Leocha, president of Travelers United, a consumer advocacy group, told Bloomberg flying in and out of London "will be an absolute horror show" if those expedited travel privileges are peeled back.
