European companies have more than Brexit to worry about

July 2, 2016 at 7:00PM

Economist

It feels indelicate to raise it at a time like this, but European business has a bigger problem on its plate than Britain's decision to leave the European Union. After a decade of stagnation, the continent's firms have suffered an alarming decline in their global clout.

Europe's slide down the corporate rankings has been brutal, even before the market rout in the wake of Brexit.

Of the 50 most valuable firms in the world, only seven are European, compared with 17 in 2006. No fewer than 31 are American, and eight are Chinese.

It's past time that Europe's bosses, investors and governments paid attention.

At the turn of the century, it seemed natural that European firms would compete head to head with American ones, dividing the world between them, especially given that Japan's once-aggressive multinationals were in retreat.

In the following years, Europe's weight rose, relative to America's, measured by the profits and value of listed firms.

But it peaked before the financial crisis.

Of the seven European firms that make the cut, three are Swiss, suggesting there really is something special about mountain air and rÃ¶sti; another, beer firm AB InBev, is run by Brazilians who happen to have picked Belgium for their main share listing.

The continent's traditional heavyweight champions have become middleweight journeymen. A European firm occupies the top spot in only one out of 24 global sectors (NestlÃ© in food).

Europe and America have economies of a similar size, but the aggregate market value of the top 500 European firms is half that of the top 500 American firms.

What went wrong? Slow growth in Europe has not helped, and a strong dollar has made American firms' domestic operations more valuable.

But four other factors also explain the slide: Europe focused on old industries such as commodities, steel and banking. The companies also focused too much on emerging markets. They stopped doing deals when the rest of the world was consolidating. And European managers' attitude toward shareholder value could account for the differences in market value in Europe and America.

To build back up, Europe will need to restrain its nationalist instincts and also temper its tougher approach to antitrust concerns. If compromises are not made, Europe's corporate weakness will eventually lead to a defensive protectionism and the continent will close itself off from the outside world.
